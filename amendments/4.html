Amendment IV

Search and Seizure


The right of the people to be secure in their persons, houses, papers, and effects, against unreasonable searches and seizures, shall not be violated, and no Warrants shall issue, but upon probable cause, supported by Oath or affirmation, and particularly describing the place to be searched, and the persons or things to be seized.

Passed by Congress September 25, 1789. Ratified December 15, 1791. Part of the first 10 amendments from the Bill of Rights

Law enfocement can't just search you for something out of nowhere. The Fourth Amendment lays out the conditions needed for a legal search or seizure for evidence related to a crime and protects citizens from unreasonable searches or seizure.

  • Officer must have a valid search warrant to search you.
    • This includes having where the search will be, a probable cause to enforce why a search is happening, the issuer of the warrant, and the judge's seal of approval.
Though protection is heavily debated, places like airports and other security places don't need it for the sake of security of the state. All illegally obtained evidence that go against the Fourth Amendment will not be used in any court case.
