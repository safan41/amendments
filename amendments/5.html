Amendment V

Rights of People in Court


No person shall be held to answer for a capital, or otherwise infamous crime, unless on a presentment or indictment of a Grand Jury, except in cases arising in the land or naval forces, or in the Militia, when in actual service in time of War or public danger; nor shall any person be subject for the same offence to be twice put in jeopardy of life or limb; nor shall be compelled in any criminal case to be a witness against himself, nor be deprived of life, liberty, or property, without due process of law; nor shall private property be taken for public use, without just compensation.

Passed by Congress September 25, 1789. Ratified December 15, 1791. Part of the first 10 amendments from the Bill of Rights

The Fifth Amendment gives citizens some basic legal protection. They include:

  • You cannot be convicted of a serious crime without the indictment of a grand jury.
  • You cannot be tried for the same crime more than twice.
    • This means if convicted, found innocent, and more evidence flows in, there still cannot be another trial.
  • You are protected from having to witness against yourself.
Two other things were mentioned in the amendment, due process and takings.

Due Process

Due process simply says that the government must act in recongnition of the legal rules and cannot go against them. This protects people from losing their life, liberty, or even propety without going through the whole legal process.

Takings

The takings clause of this amendment was the phrase "nor shall private property be taken for public use, without just compensation." This has many understandings to go towards the common idea that the government cannot take private property unless it is aquired for "public use" and if the government pays back "just compensation [money]".
